# Homepage Implementation Plan

## Purpose
The homepage should act as the student platform’s primary entry point for Montgomery College.

It must feel:
- premium
- modern
- trustworthy
- campus-appropriate
- easy to scan
- useful on first glance

It should **not** feel like:
- a futuristic product demo
- a flashy startup landing page
- a generic SaaS homepage
- a social feed like Facebook

That matters because college organizations and campus buyers are usually evaluating for clarity, familiarity, and trust. If the homepage looks overly polished in a SaaS way, it can read as unfamiliar or too “salesy” for institutional adoption.

## Audience Context
Primary audiences:
- students
- club officers
- campus staff
- institutional decision-makers

What they need to feel quickly:
- this is a real campus platform
- it is organized
- it is easy to use daily
- it is not experimental
- it is not trying too hard to look trendy

## Visual Direction
Use a design language that feels:
- clean
- spacious
- institutional
- product-quality
- restrained

Avoid:
- neon effects
- glassmorphism
- glowing gradients
- oversized hero gimmicks
- dense card stacks
- decorative motion that does not help navigation
- “SaaS launch page” aesthetics

The homepage should feel like a serious student engagement platform, not a marketing website.

## Homepage Structure
The homepage should follow this order:

1. Hero / First Impression
2. Upcoming Events
3. Active Clubs & Community
4. Campus Resources
5. Support & Help CTA
6. Footer

Each section should support a clear student task.

## Section Plan

### 1. Hero / First Impression
Goals:
- explain what the platform is immediately
- establish the tone for the rest of the page
- make the page feel trustworthy and current
- support the mobile-first nature of the product without over-emphasizing the device mockup

Recommended content:
- clear headline
- short supporting paragraph
- primary CTA buttons
- one rounded product image or device preview
- small trust signals or metrics

Design rules:
- keep it open and balanced
- use strong hierarchy
- do not overcrowd the first screen
- do not make the preview the entire story
- do not make the layout look like a SaaS hero with exaggerated feature marketing

The hero should say: “This is the place students go first.”

### 2. Upcoming Events
Goals:
- help students quickly see what is happening next
- make scanning easy
- make date and time obvious
- keep cards compact

Recommended content:
- concise event cards
- image only if helpful
- title
- date/time
- location
- host club or office
- RSVP or view action

Design rules:
- cards should be short and scannable
- avoid large event posters taking over the section
- prioritize clarity over visual drama
- keep the grid compact and consistent

The section should feel like an operational calendar preview, not an event marketplace.

### 3. Active Clubs & Community
Goals:
- show that clubs are active
- help discovery without clutter
- encourage joining and exploration

Recommended content:
- club cards
- active clubs count
- clear category or campus tags
- short descriptions
- join or view action

Design rules:
- keep the section structured and easy to scan
- show enough identity to distinguish organizations
- do not make it feel like a social discovery feed

This should feel like a student organization directory with energy, not a community timeline.

### 4. Campus Resources
Goals:
- surface real campus tools
- help students move fast
- reduce hunting through menus or external sites

Recommended content:
- MyMC Portal
- Blackboard support
- library
- campus maps
- student services
- alert/safety links

Design rules:
- present resources as useful links, not decorative tiles
- keep copy short and practical
- avoid making this section look like a SaaS “features” grid

This section should feel institutional and functional.

### 5. Support & Help CTA
Goals:
- provide a clear path when students need assistance
- feel calm, reliable, and official

Recommended content:
- support page link
- login/help link
- issue reporting guidance
- contact or help center access

Design rules:
- keep the tone reassuring
- make the next step obvious
- avoid heavy marketing language

This section should read like a help desk entry point, not a promotional banner.

### 6. Footer
Goals:
- close the page with credibility
- keep official links and navigation available

Recommended content:
- campus identity
- quick links
- support links
- legal/privacy links

Design rules:
- simple
- readable
- not crowded
- not branded like a startup footer

## Content Rules
Homepage copy should be:
- short
- useful
- direct
- campus-aware
- non-hypey

Avoid copy that sounds like:
- “revolutionary”
- “next-gen”
- “magic”
- “AI-powered experience”
- “supercharged”
- “modern productivity platform”

Use language that feels like:
- student home
- campus resources
- official support
- upcoming events
- active clubs
- campus life

## UI Rules
### Use cards only when they add value
Cards are useful for:
- events
- clubs
- resources

Cards are not useful when they:
- create unnecessary containers
- make the page feel boxed-in
- turn everything into isolated panels

### Favor open layouts
Use open layouts for:
- hero
- section headers
- support CTA
- page rhythm

### Keep rhythm consistent
Spacing should feel:
- calm
- even
- intentional
- easy to move through

## What To Avoid
Do not make the homepage look like:
- a fintech dashboard
- a startup SaaS homepage
- a crypto product
- a flashy design system demo
- a social media feed

That style can be visually impressive, but it is a red flag for many college organizations because it feels unfamiliar and overly commercial.

## Implementation Order
Recommended sequence:

1. Finalize hero layout and image treatment
2. Lock section order and spacing
3. Tighten event cards for scanability
4. Refine club discovery blocks
5. Keep resources and support sections minimal and practical
6. Review typography and button treatment across the page
7. Validate mobile behavior after the desktop structure is solid

## Success Criteria
The homepage is successful if:
- a student can understand the platform in a few seconds
- events and clubs are obvious
- the page feels trustworthy to campus staff and buyers
- nothing feels gimmicky or overly styled
- the page feels like a daily-use campus tool

If the page looks “impressive” but not believable, it is failing the product goal.
